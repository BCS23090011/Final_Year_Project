Dashboard 2 — How Do They Play? (In-Game Behaviour)

Event participation, task completion, and Nod-Krai region satisfaction ratings reveal the engagement landscape. Players who complete all major activities show significantly higher retention intent — directly confirming the SHAP finding that event participation is the #1 retention predictor. Among the six Nod-Krai dimensions, Combat scores the lowest across all play duration groups — a telling signal that players find the combat formula repetitive and formulaic: new characters every patch, but the same underlying rotations and mechanics.

Notably, veteran players (3–5 years) rate Story and Atmosphere higher than newer players — the opposite of what repetition fatigue would predict. This is explained by Nod-Krai's region design: the map is significantly more compact than older regions, making chest hunting and exploration far less time-consuming. The reward-to-time ratio improves noticeably compared to sprawling regions like Sumeru or Fontaine, meaning veterans — who have endured far worse exploration fatigue before — recognise Nod-Krai as a genuine step forward and rate it accordingly.

Dashboard 3 — Why Do They Stay? (Motivations & Desires)

Character design (41 mentions) is the single biggest retention driver — players invest emotionally in characters, making new character releases a powerful re-engagement mechanism. World exploration (31) and content updates (27) follow, revealing that lore-driven players represent the most stable retention segment. For desired improvements: generous rewards (43), free pulls (38), and better anniversary events (37) dominate — pointing directly to the monetisation system as the greatest source of friction.

Churned Player Dashboards

Dashboard 4 — Who Left? (Churn Demographics)

Churned players show a notably different demographic fingerprint from active players. Mobile dominates as the platform of departure — suggesting mobile-specific UX friction (battery drain, controls, performance) may compound content-driven churn. The churn population is segmented into three archetypes: Watching & Waiting (~36%), Passively Churned (~41%), and Permanently Lost (~23%).

Dashboard 5 — Why Did They Leave? (Top Churn Factors)

The top churn reasons reveal two distinct patterns: situational churn (no time, study/work pressure) vs design-driven churn (exploration fatigue, story not engaging). Critically, most churn is not permanent — situational churners (the majority) remain potential re-engagement targets. Only the "permanently lost" archetype cites complete loss of interest.

Dashboard 6 — What Brings Them Back? (Return Conditions)

The return conditions data is highly actionable for game developers. Generous rewards (18) and free pulls (14) both point to the same root cause: players feel the reward-to-effort ratio is too low. Critically, the top active player desire (generous rewards, 43 mentions) exactly matches the top churn player return condition — this cross-group alignment is the strongest design recommendation in the entire study.
